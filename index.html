01 // THE BASICS

What IS
Formula 1?

🏎️
The Sport

Formula 1 is the highest class of single-seater motorsport, governed by the FIA. "Formula" refers to a set of rules that all cars must follow. Teams design, build, and race these machines across a global season.

SINCE 1950
🏆
Two Championships

There are two titles at stake each season: the Drivers' Championship (for the individual pilot) and the Constructors' Championship (for the team that builds the car). Points are awarded at every race.

DUAL TITLES
📍
The Calendar

Races are called Grand Prix events. The season spans ~24 races across 5 continents — from Monaco's streets to Suzuka's curves to Las Vegas' strip. Each Grand Prix spans a whole weekend.

GLOBAL SERIES
⚡
The Technology

F1 cars are engineering marvels — hybrid power units producing ~1000 hp, aerodynamic bodies generating more downforce than their own weight, and tyres that are the critical connection to the track.

CUTTING EDGE
📊
Points System

Top 10 finishers score points: 1st = 25 pts, 2nd = 18, 3rd = 15... down to 10th = 1 pt. A bonus point goes to the fastest lap (if the driver is in the top 10). Sprint races award smaller point hauls.

25-18-15-12-10-8-6-4-2-1
🛞
Tyre Compounds

Pirelli supplies all teams with tyres in different compounds: Soft (fastest, least durable), Medium, and Hard. At least two must be used in a race. Strategy revolves around when to pit and swap them.

02 // THE RACE WEEKEND

How A Weekend
Unfolds

Thursday
Media & Prep Day

Drivers and team principals face the press in the paddock. Engineers analyze track data, set up simulations, and brief their drivers. No running on track.

Friday
Practice 1 & 2

Two 60-minute practice sessions. Teams test setups, gather tyre data, and run new components. FP1 often sees young drivers take a seat. FP2 is crucial for long-run pace.

Saturday AM
Practice 3

Final 60-min practice before qualifying. Teams lock in their setups and drivers warm up for the afternoon's pressure session. Short, focused, purposeful.

Saturday PM
Qualifying ⚡

Three knockout rounds (Q1, Q2, Q3) determine Sunday's grid. Q1 eliminates the slowest 5, Q2 removes 5 more, and Q3 is a ten-driver shootout for pole position. One lap. Maximum attack.

Sunday
Race Day 🏁

The main event! ~305km (or ~2 hours, whichever comes first). Formation lap, standing start, and then chaos, strategy, overtakes, pit stops, safety cars, and — hopefully — drama to the chequered flag.

04 // THE RULES

What You
Need to Know

🚩 Flags & Their Meanings ▸
  • 🟡 Yellow: Hazard ahead. No overtaking. Reduce speed.
  • 🔴 Red: Race stopped. Return to pit lane immediately.
  • 🔵 Blue: Let the faster car behind you pass. (Lap traffic)
  • ⚫ Black & White: Warning for unsportsmanlike conduct.
  • ⬛ Black: You are disqualified. Return to pits.
  • 🏁 Chequered: Race over! You've finished!
🛡️ Safety Car & VSC ▸

When there's an incident on track, the Safety Car (SC) is deployed — all drivers must queue up behind it and maintain position, no overtaking. Pit stops during SC periods are strategic gold. A Virtual Safety Car (VSC) slows everyone by the same percentage without a physical car — less dramatic but equally tactical.

🔧 DRS — The Overtaking Aid ▸

DRS (Drag Reduction System) opens a flap on the rear wing, cutting drag and adding ~10-15 km/h straight-line speed. It can only be activated in designated DRS zones and only if you're within 1 second of the car in front at the detection point. Controversial but exciting.

⏱️ Penalties & Stewards ▸

Race stewards can hand out penalties for incidents. Common ones include:

  • 5-second penalty: served during a pit stop
  • 10-second stop-go: you must stop in the pits for 10 seconds
  • Grid drop: start X places further back next race
  • Drive-through: drive through the pit lane without stopping
💰 Budget Cap & Parc Fermé ▸

Since 2021, teams are limited to a $135M cost cap per season (excluding driver salaries and marketing). Once qualifying ends, cars enter Parc Fermé — a strict condition where teams can only make limited changes to the car before the race. This limits how much teams can adjust their setup overnight.

🏎️ Car Regulations ▸

F1 cars must comply with strict technical regulations covering: minimum weight (798kg including driver), specific power unit rules (1.6L V6 turbo-hybrid), aerodynamic boundaries defined by the FIA, and standard parts mandated for all teams. The regulations are rewritten periodically — the last major overhaul was 2022.

05 // THE LANGUAGE

Sound Like
A Fan

Apex

The innermost point of a corner. Hitting the apex correctly is key to carrying maximum speed through a turn.

Undercut

Pitting before your rival to gain track position. Fresh tyres let you set fast laps while they stay out on older rubber.

Overcut

Staying out longer than your rival, letting them pit first and then using the gap to pit from a better position.

Pole Position

Starting from the front of the grid, earned by posting the fastest time in Q3 qualifying.

Graining

When rubber from the tyre tears off in small pieces, covering the surface and reducing grip. Ruins your lap time until the tyre "comes in."

Marbles

Bits of rubber that build up off the racing line. Drive over them and your grip disappears instantly. Very dangerous.

Flatspot

Locking a wheel under braking wears one spot flat. Causes a horrible vibration through the car and is terrible for laptime.

Stint

A continuous period of racing between pit stops. A one-stop race has two stints. Two-stop = three stints.

Paddock

The behind-the-scenes area at a circuit where team motorhomes and transporters are parked. VIP access only.

Parc Fermé

French for "closed park." The secure area where cars are held after qualifying. Minimal changes allowed until the race.

Backmarker

A car being lapped by the leaders. They must yield to leading cars (indicated by blue flags) — but when and how can affect race strategy.

DNF

Did Not Finish. The two most dreaded letters in motorsport. Engine failure, accident, or any retirement from the race.
